The Stomach

Your stomach is a muscular organ that digests food. It is part of your gastrointestinal (GI) tract. It contracts and produces acids and enzymes that break down food before passing it to your small intestine.

Overview

The stomach is a J-shaped organ that digests food by producing enzymes and acids. This mixture breaks food down so it can pass to your small intestine. It is part of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, a long tube that runs from your mouth to your anus.

  • Temporarily stores food
  • Contracts and relaxes to mix and break down food
  • Produces enzymes and specialized cells to digest food

How does the stomach work with the rest of the GI tract?

Each part of your GI tract helps break down food and carry nutrients through your body. After nutrients and water are absorbed, waste is expelled through the large intestine.

  • Mouth: Food is chewed, swallowed, and directed by the epiglottis.
  • Esophagus: Pushes food to the stomach.
  • Stomach: Creates digestive juices and breaks down food.
  • Small intestine: Absorbs nutrients and water.
  • Large intestine: Turns waste into stool.
  • Rectum: Stores stool until a bowel movement.

Anatomy

The stomach sits in the upper abdomen, on the left side of the body. It connects to the esophagus at the top and the small intestine at the bottom.

The stomach has five sections: cardia, fundus, body, antrum, and pylorus.

Structure

  • Mucosa: Inner lining with folds (rugae).
  • Submucosa: Connective tissue, blood vessels, and nerves.
  • Muscularis externa: Three muscle layers for digestion.
  • Serosa: Outer membrane covering the stomach.

Conditions and Disorders

Common conditions that affect the stomach include:

  • Gastric ulcers
  • Gastritis
  • GERD (acid reflux)
  • Gastroparesis
  • Indigestion
  • Peptic ulcer disease
  • Stomach cancer

Care

You can keep your stomach healthy by:

  • Drinking alcohol only in moderation
  • Staying hydrated
  • Eating enough fiber
  • Exercising regularly
  • Limiting processed foods
  • Managing stress
  • Quitting smoking or tobacco

Additional Common Questions

Ask your doctor if you have chronic symptoms:

  • What is the most likely cause of my symptoms?
  • What tests are needed for diagnosis?
  • Are there OTC medications that can help?
  • Should I make changes to my diet?

When should I call my doctor?

Seek medical help if you have:

  • Chest pain
  • Fever
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Blood in stool
  • Severe dehydration or diarrhea
  • Sudden, intense abdominal pain

A note from Cleveland Clinic

The stomach is a muscular organ that contracts, relaxes, and produces acids to break down food. You can support its health by eating well, exercising, and managing stress.
