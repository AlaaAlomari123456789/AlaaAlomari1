The Heart

The heart serves as a pump to circulate blood throughout the body. It has four chambers in humans: two atria and two ventricles. The heart is located between the lungs, slightly left of center, behind the breastbone, resting on the diaphragm.

The heart consists of myocardium (muscular wall), pericardium (outer layer), and endocardium (inner lining). The upper chambers (atria) receive blood, and the lower chambers (ventricles) pump it out. Valves ensure one-way blood flow.

Blood passes through two circuits: pulmonary circulation (right atrium → lungs → left atrium) and systemic circulation (left atrium → body → right atrium). Blood pressure is highest in the left ventricle and aorta, decreasing through capillaries and veins.

The heartbeat is caused by electrical impulses from the sinoatrial (SA) node, which triggers atria contraction, followed by ventricles contraction via the atrioventricular (AV) node. The cycle includes systole (contraction) and diastole (relaxation). Average adult resting heart rate: 70 bpm.

The "lub-dup" sounds are caused by valve closures. Murmurs may indicate valve leakage. Coronary heart disease results from blocked arteries, reducing oxygen to myocardium, and is a leading cause of death worldwide.

Biomarkers indicate physiological states or diseases. Echocardiography uses ultrasound to image the heart, assess chamber size, valve structure, and detect abnormalities. Early diagnosis via biomarkers improves treatment outcomes.

René Laënnec (1781–1826) invented the stethoscope, enabling mediate auscultation. He studied heart and lung sounds, developed diagnostic techniques, and contributed significantly to clinical medicine.
