The Kidneys

The kidneys are two bean-shaped organs, each about the size of a fist, located just below the rib cage, one on each side of your spine. They filter blood, remove wastes, and regulate fluids in your body.

The kidneys remove wastes, extra fluid, and acid from the blood. They maintain the balance of water, salts, and minerals (sodium, calcium, phosphorus, potassium), produce hormones controlling blood pressure, make red blood cells, and keep bones healthy.

Each kidney has about a million filtering units called nephrons. The glomerulus filters blood, and the tubule returns needed substances while removing wastes, forming urine. Blood enters via the renal artery and leaves through the renal vein.

Blood flows into the glomerulus, a cluster of tiny blood vessels. Small molecules, wastes, and mostly water pass into the tubule, while proteins and blood cells remain in the blood.

The tubule reabsorbs water, minerals, and nutrients back into the blood and removes excess acid. Remaining fluid and wastes become urine.

The NIDDK and NIH support research into kidney diseases. Clinical trials test new ways to prevent, detect, or treat kidney conditions.
